The Bridegroom

· · ·

The entire Bible is a love story. You are the one being pursued.

This is not a metaphor. It is the architecture of reality. From the moment God formed humanity from the dust, He has been seeking a bride. Through the patriarchs and the prophets, through the fire on Sinai and the silence of the exile, the Bridegroom has been walking toward us.

He does not want subjects. He does not want employees. He wants intimacy. The Bridegroom: The Desire of God traces this single, blazing thread through the entire canon of Scripture, revealing how every covenant is a betrothal, and every prophecy is a love letter.

He is standing in the dew. He is knocking. Where are you?

The Name

"In that day, declares the LORD, you will call me 'My Husband,' and no longer will you call me 'My Master.'"
— Hosea 2:16

אִישִׁי (Ishi) means 'my husband.' It comes from Hosea 2:16, where God tells the bride: you will no longer call me Ba'ali — my lord, my master, my owner. You will call me Ishi — my man. My husband. The one whose body is the origin of your body. The fire does not want to be obeyed. The fire wants to be loved.

עוֹלָם (Olam) means 'forever.' It comes from Hosea 2:19: 'I will betroth you to me forever.' The desire has no expiration. The betrothal has no end.

וְ (The vav) — the prefix — is the Hebrew conjunction 'and.' In biblical Hebrew, the vav often initiates action: 'And God said.' 'And it was evening.' The Bridegroom moves first. The bride responds. The vav is the step the Bridegroom takes before the bride takes hers.

There is no face behind this name. No biography. No credentials offered. Only a voice, and the Voice it carries. The work exists to make one thing visible: that the God of the Bible is a Bridegroom, and His desire for you is the oldest fire in the cosmos, and it has not gone out.
